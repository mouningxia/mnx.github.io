The Empty Corner

The discovery was made not in the urgency of daylight, but in the quiet confusion of a winter's night. On Tuesday evening, as a light snow dusted the campus, my roommate was returning from the dining hall. Crossing the bridge that connects it to our women's dormitory‚Äî a common shortcut and a known gathering spot for the campus cats‚Äîshe noticed something amiss. A familiar cat, one we often saw nestled in a nearby shelter, was hunched and alert hunched and alert outdoors in the cold. Puzzled, she went to check its usual refuge. It was gone.

She brought the news back to our dorm. By then, curfew was approaching. There was little to be done in the dark and the cold but share the disquieting update within our circle and wait. The following morning, between classes, a small group of us made our way back to the bridge toback to the bridge to confirm what she had seen. The scene was stark: not a single shelter remained in the area. Only compressed patches in the grass and faint impressions in the light snow hinted at what had snow hinted at what had been there.

The initial flurry of messages among friends gave way to a more systematic, sober assessment. The disappearance was not isolated. Over the course of the morning, reports trickled in from across campus: shelters were also missing near the back gate and several other familiar spots. The scale of the loss was becoming clear.

The Question Left Behind

The vanished shelters, scattered across key campus crossroads, left a void filled with questions. The bridge, a daily thoroughfare for students heading to meals or returning home, had been more than a transit point ‚Äîit was a recognized micro-habitat.

"If the shelters were moved by security or other staff to a safer, warmer place, I could understand," said Liu Zhe, a sophomore who frequents the area. "But if this was done deliberately by someone, I truly feel furious."

Online and in dorm conversations, reactions ranged from sorrow to anger, yet they converged into a shared willingness to act..

The Campus Cat Care Group responded swiftly. "Our top priority was the immediate safety of the cats," explained the group's coordinator. Volunteers built makeshift shelters from whatever materials they could find and began searching for any cats showing signs of stress or displacement. "We focused on reassurance‚Äîfamiliar food, a gentle presence‚Äîto restore a sense of security," she added.

However, the coordinated removal of shelters across multiple locations pointed to reasons beyond a simple cleanup. "The silence from official channels is what unsettles people the most," noted a long-term volunteer. "It turns care into a form of uncertainty."

Faced with a systemic removal rather than an isolated incident, the Campus Cat Care Group's strategy has evolved from immediate response to more practical planning. Their focus is now on concrete protective measures, hoping to gather more goodwill across campus.

From Temporary to Official: Clear Signage

"We hope the new cat shelters won't be moved arbitrarily," the coordinator stated. The group plans to equip all newly placed shelters with eye-catching signs, clearly stating they are winter facilities for campus stray cats, accompanied by a warm reminder: "Please do not move, thank you for your care."

"A clear sign helps passersby understand this isn't waste, but a small home that needs collective upkeep," she explained.

Practical Actions: Translating Goodwill into Tangible Support

Beyond building sturdier shelters, the group is working to turn student concern into concrete support:

Setting Up Simple Donation Points:After obtaining permission, establish fixed locations to receive voluntary donations from students, such as old blankets, foam pads, and other warming materials.

Creating Care Guides:Post simple instructions near shelters, providing basic guidance on how to feed cats scientifically in winter to avoid frozen food or environmental contamination.

Maintaining Daily Patrols:Organize volunteers to regularly check the condition of the shelters and promptly comfort and care for the cats.

A Call for Collective Responsibility

"This incident showed us that many students are willing to do something for the cats," the coordinator concluded. "Our small goal is to get the cats safely through the winter; our greater hope is to work with everyone to make our shared campus a kinder, warmer place."

The picture they paint is not complex: some sturdy shelters, some understanding glances, a share of lasting care.

When the new shelters are built, they will carry more than just warmth; they will also become a silent invitation, asking everyone to become a guardian of goodwill on this land.
